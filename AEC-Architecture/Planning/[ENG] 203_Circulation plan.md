<h1 align="center">Circulation planning</h1>
<h3 align="center">― Understanding user, service, and egress circulation changes how you read a plan</h3>

<p align="center">When looking at a building plan,</p>
<p align="center">you may feel “something is uncomfortable” or “the circulation is odd”</p>
<p align="center">even more than the room layout or size.</p>

<p align="center">In those cases, the problem is often not the size of the space,</p>
<p align="center">but the way people move—its circulation.</p>

<p align="center">Circulation is not always visible,</p>
<p align="center">yet users move along it every day and experience the space through it.</p>

<p align="center">That’s why circulation is one of the most basic and important concepts in plan design.</p>
<p align="center">Knowing the characteristics of different circulation types greatly helps in understanding plans.</p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">1. Circulation is not a “path,” but people’s movement</h2>

<p align="center">Many non-specialists think circulation</p>
<p align="center">is just a visible “path” like a corridor.</p>

<p align="center">But in architecture, circulation is a much broader concept.</p>

<p align="center">Circulation means</p>
<p align="center"><strong>who moves, for what purpose, and from where to where</strong>.</p>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> The path from the entrance to the living room</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> The flow from an office desk to a meeting room</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> The route to exit the building safely in an emergency</p>

<p align="center">All of these are circulation.</p>

<p align="center">In short, circulation is not a line,</p>
<p align="center"><strong>but a flow drawn by people’s behavior</strong>.</p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">2. User circulation</h2>

<p align="center">User circulation is the everyday route of occupants and visitors—</p>
<p align="center"><strong>the movement of people who use the building</strong>.</p>
<p align="center">It largely determines convenience and first impressions.</p>

<p align="center">
	<img src="201-203_img/03_image1.png" alt="User circulation image" width="70%" />
</p>

<h3 align="center">✔️ What matters in user circulation</h3>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Is the route from entrance to destination intuitive?</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Is there unnecessary detouring?</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Are private and public spaces naturally separated?</p>

<p align="center">Good user circulation</p>
<p align="center">lets people move naturally <strong>without having to think</strong>.</p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">3. Service circulation: invisible but essential</h2>

<p align="center">Service circulation includes cleaning, maintenance, logistics, and staff movement—</p>
<p align="center"><strong>not for users directly, but still essential</strong>.</p>

<p align="center">This circulation should be unobtrusive and,</p>
<p align="center">as much as possible, not overlap with user circulation.</p>

<p align="center">
	<img src="201-203_img/flow.jpg" alt="Service circulation image" width="70%" />
</p>

<h3 align="center">✔️ Why service circulation matters</h3>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Overlap with user circulation causes discomfort</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Reduces privacy and spatial quality</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Lowers operational efficiency</p>

<p align="center">That’s why designers aim for</p>
<p align="center"><strong>“circulation that works well without being seen.”</strong></p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">4. Egress circulation</h2>

<p align="center">Egress circulation is the route for escaping the building safely</p>
<p align="center">during emergencies like fire or disasters.</p>

<p align="center">Here, convenience is less important than <strong>clarity and intuitiveness</strong>.</p>

<p align="center">
	<img src="201-203_img/Evacuation.png" alt="Egress circulation image 1" width="70%" />
</p>

<p align="center">
	<img src="201-203_img/Circulation plan4.png" alt="Egress circulation image 2" width="70%" />
</p>

<h3 align="center">✔️ Key points of egress circulation</h3>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Must be understandable at a glance</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> No dead ends</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Always available for use</p>

<p align="center">Normally, people barely notice it,</p>
<p align="center">but in emergencies it becomes <strong>the most important circulation</strong>.</p>

<h3 align="center">✔️ Extra info!</h3>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> The maximum distance to an exterior-access stair must be within 30 m.</p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">5. Good plans organize circulation naturally</h2>

<p align="center">In a good plan,</p>
<p align="center">circulation is organized without forcing it.</p>

<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> User circulation feels comfortable</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Service circulation stays hidden</p>
<p align="center"><strong>●</strong> Egress circulation is clear</p>

<p align="center">In such plans,</p>
<p align="center">paths are readable even without signage,</p>
<p align="center">and first-time visitors won’t get lost.</p>

<hr />

<h2 align="center">Closing</h2>

<p align="center">Circulation is not the most eye-catching element on drawings,</p>
<p align="center">nor is it the most visible.</p>

<p align="center">But users move along it every day and evaluate the space through it.</p>

<p align="center">&gt; To say a circulation is good</p>
<p align="center">&gt; means <strong>this space is taking care of me</strong>.</p>
